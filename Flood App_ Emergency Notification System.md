# **Engineering a Sensorless, Real-Time Flood Warning and Emergency Response Mobile Platform**

## **Executive Overview and Architectural Paradigm**

The escalating frequency and severity of urban flash floods, driven by anthropogenically accelerated climate change, present an unprecedented challenge to municipal emergency management and civilian safety. Traditional flood monitoring infrastructure relies heavily on networks of physical Internet of Things (IoT) sensors, stream gauges, and telemetry stations. While highly accurate at their specific deployment sites, these physical networks are encumbered by prohibitive capital expenditures, continuous maintenance requirements, vulnerability to physical destruction during the very extreme weather events they are designed to monitor, and vast spatial blind spots where sensor coverage is absent.

To overcome these inherent physical limitations, the development of a street-level, real-time flood warning mobile application operating entirely without localized IoT sensors represents a paradigm shift in disaster informatics. By circumventing physical hardware dependencies, this proposed system relies on the ingestion of hyper-local meteorological Application Programming Interfaces (APIs), high-resolution topographical matrices, and historical satellite imagery to dynamically model and predict flood inundation extents. This approach shifts the operational burden entirely from hardware deployment and maintenance to computational scalability, data pipeline orchestration, and advanced algorithmic accuracy.

This comprehensive report examines the deep architectural foundations, machine learning pipelines, and mobile operating system constraints required to construct and deploy such a system. The core backend relies on the FastAPI Python framework to handle asynchronous, high-throughput client requests, coupled intimately with the advanced geospatial indexing and percolator capabilities of Elasticsearch. By analyzing decades of historical satellite data—primarily from the Copernicus Sentinel missions and the MODIS Global Flood Database—the system identifies historically vulnerable geographic areas, classified within the system architecture as "blue zones".1

Furthermore, this analysis investigates the technical feasibility and legal constraints of implementing a localized emergency response flow. The user query proposes a mechanism designed to protect individuals trapped within active flood zones: if a user opens the application within a confirmed inundated area, the system either automatically initiates a programmatic call to the local emergency dispatch number (e.g., 911, 112\) or dispatches peer-to-peer push notifications to nearby users. This report critically evaluates the absolute boundaries of mobile operating system permissions, highlighting the strict architectural limitations imposed by both iOS and Android on programmatic emergency dialing, and proposes robust, technically viable alternative workflows utilizing peer-to-peer High-Priority and Critical Push Notifications.3

## **Delineation of "Blue Zones" via Historical Satellite Telemetry**

The foundational layer of a completely sensorless flood prediction model is the empirical mapping of historically inundated geographic areas. Because the system lacks real-time physical water level gauges, it must compute the probability of urban and rural flooding based on how the localized topography has previously reacted to severe precipitation and hydrological events. Establishing this baseline requires processing massive archives of Earth observation data.

### **Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) and Optical Satellite Integration**

To accurately map historical flood extents and define the static "blue zones," the system must ingest and process vast archives of satellite telemetry. Optical sensors, such as those deployed aboard the Copernicus Sentinel-2 mission, provide multi-spectral imagery with a high revisit time and a wide 290-kilometer swath width.1 Sentinel-2 data is highly valuable for distinguishing the distinct spectral signatures of turbid, sediment-rich floodwaters against dry land and vegetation. However, optical imagery is fundamentally and unavoidably limited by atmospheric interference; severe flooding events are invariably accompanied by dense, prolonged cloud cover, rendering optical satellites practically blind during the critical phases of a hydrological disaster.

To resolve this atmospheric limitation, the system relies predominantly on Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) data, specifically derived from the Copernicus Sentinel-1 mission.6 SAR instruments operate in the microwave domain of the electromagnetic spectrum, allowing the radar pulses to penetrate dense cloud cover and operate entirely independently of solar illumination, providing day-and-night observation capabilities. In SAR imagery, calm water bodies behave as specular reflectors for the incident radar pulses. The microwave energy is scattered forward and away from the satellite sensor, resulting in very low backscatter returns and distinctly dark pixels in the generated imagery.7

However, the extraction of accurate flood boundaries from SAR imagery presents a non-trivial computer vision challenge, particularly in complex urban environments. In cities, the geometric configuration of buildings and flooded streets can create a "double-bounce" scattering effect. The radar pulse bounces off the water surface and strikes the vertical face of a building before returning directly to the sensor, artificially brightening the radar return and masking the presence of water.7 Advanced feature boundary detection algorithms must be deployed to counter this phenomenon. Historically, the Joint Research Centre (JRC) has utilized the Snake algorithm, a statistical active contour technique. This algorithm searches the image space utilizing a dynamic curvilinear contour until it settles on feature boundaries, maintaining high spatial resolution while significantly mitigating the characteristic speckle noise inherent to SAR imagery.8

### **Machine Learning for Raster-to-Vector Polygon Extraction**

To utilize historical flood extents within an Elasticsearch geospatial environment, the raw raster data (grids of pixels) from satellite imagery must be transformed into highly precise vector polygons (GeoJSON format). Recent advancements in deep learning, specifically convolutional neural networks (CNNs), have revolutionized this extraction pipeline, moving beyond traditional thresholding techniques.9

The U-Net architecture, initially developed for biomedical image segmentation, has proven exceptionally adept at semantic segmentation of satellite imagery. The network consists of a contracting path (encoder) to capture context and an expanding path (decoder) that enables precise localization, connected by skip connections that preserve spatial hierarchies. Research indicates that a bi-temporal U-Net model—which simultaneously analyzes pre-flood and post-flood SAR imagery—significantly outperforms traditional methods by isolating the specific change vector associated with the flooding event, ignoring permanent water bodies and static shadows.10

By integrating high-resolution Digital Elevation Model (DEM) data alongside Sentinel-1 SAR bands, the U-Net model achieves vastly superior flood inundation mapping. Empirical studies have demonstrated that the inclusion of topographical elevation data improves the median F1 score of the segmentation output from 0.62 to 0.73.11 In specific catastrophic events, optimized Attention U-Net architectures operating on Sentinel-1 cross-polarization (VH) datasets have demonstrated F1-scores as high as 0.89.12 Furthermore, specialized architectures like the GAC-UNET model leverage transfer learning and model reprogramming to achieve a 91% mean Average Precision (mAP) and an 89% Intersection over Union (IoU) score for flood area segmentation.13

Once the deep learning model outputs a probabilistic raster map indicating the flood extent, geospatial libraries such as GDAL (Geospatial Data Abstraction Library) and Shapely are utilized within the Python backend to vectorize the output. The gdal.FPolygonize function effectively and rapidly converts floating-point raster arrays into continuous, mathematically defined Shapely polygons.14 These polygons are subsequently generalized using algorithms like the Douglas-Peucker algorithm to reduce the overall vertex count without losing critical topological fidelity. This vertex reduction is mandatory to render the polygons computationally suitable for indexing in Elasticsearch and for transmission over mobile networks.2

To bootstrap the system globally, the architecture ingests pre-computed datasets such as the Global Flood Database. This database contains validated maps of the extent and temporal distribution of 913 major flood events occurring between 2000 and 2018, generated using NASA's MODIS (Aqua and Terra) satellites.2 While MODIS data has a relatively low spatial resolution (250 meters), it provides an unparalleled two-decade historical baseline.2 By intersecting Dartmouth Flood Observatory polygons with HydroSHEDS watersheds, the Global Flood Database provides a massive repository of "blue zone" GeoJSON files that can be directly seeded into the Elasticsearch cluster.2

## **Real-Time Meteorological Ingestion and Predictive Modeling**

Because the proposed architecture relies entirely on external data feeds for real-time alerting triggers rather than physical ground sensors, the selection, ingestion, and normalization of meteorological data must be exceptionally granular and robust. The application continuously correlates live atmospheric conditions against the historically established blue zones to forecast the imminent onset of street-level flooding.

### **Hyper-Local Weather API Integration Architecture**

To achieve street-level predictive accuracy, standard regional weather forecasts are vastly insufficient. The architecture must integrate real-time, high-resolution Application Programming Interfaces (APIs) capable of delivering hyper-local meteorological telemetry.

Providers such as Meteomatics offer highly advanced, downscaled weather models with a 1-kilometer spatial resolution (the US1k model). This level of granularity is capable of identifying volatile micro-weather patterns and intense, highly localized precipitation cells that standard government-issued 9-kilometer resolution models completely miss.18 Extreme weather events are becoming more volatile, and the ability to detect precipitation density within a single square kilometer is the difference between a successful warning and a failure to alert.18

OpenWeatherMap's One Call API 3.0 provides an alternative or supplemental data stream, offering minute-by-minute forecasting for the next hour, daily forecasts, and government-issued national weather alerts (e.g., from NOAA and EUMETNET).19 Furthermore, Tomorrow.io supplements these streams with extensive historical climate APIs and real-time monitoring of predefined geospatial locations up to 14 days in advance.20 Finally, specialized emergency management APIs like FloodMapp generate asset-level flood forecasts and 1-meter high-resolution impact maps.21 By combining these APIs, the backend constructs a continuous, multidimensional matrix of current and projected atmospheric conditions over the designated blue zones.

### **Sensorless Flash Flood Prediction Machine Learning Models**

Predicting the exact onset and spread of an urban flash flood without real-time physical water level telemetry requires tightly coupling the ingested meteorological API data with static topographical and hydrological variables. Relying purely on physical, physics-based hydrodynamic models (such as solving the shallow water equations) is highly computationally intensive, requires extensive parameter calibration, and is fundamentally unsuitable for generating real-time predictions for thousands of concurrent mobile app users.22 Therefore, the system utilizes data-driven machine learning models optimized for rapid inference.

The predictive architecture is typically structured as a hybrid machine learning pipeline. The model ingests a variety of static features, primarily derived from geographic information systems (GIS). These include high-resolution Digital Elevation Models (DEM), distance to natural river networks, distance to man-made urban drainage grids, and the percentage of impervious land cover (which dictates the rate of surface water runoff).23 Live precipitation rates, cumulative rainfall projections from the weather APIs, and satellite-derived soil moisture proxy data serve as the dynamic inputs.25

Extensive academic studies evaluating various machine learning algorithms for urban flood prediction have consistently identified tree-based ensemble methods—specifically Random Forest (RF) and eXtreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost)—as highly effective, often outperforming deep neural networks for tabular geospatial data.23 In a comparative analysis simulating urban flood control engineering systems, the XGBoost algorithm provided the most stable, accurate, and computationally efficient simulation outcomes. The model yielded a Nash coefficient (![][image1]) greater than 0.87 and relative error (RE) values strictly below 0.06.26

XGBoost's success in this domain stems from its algorithmic ability to handle highly complex, non-linear relationships between intense rainfall bursts and localized depression zones identified in the DEM. It can effectively nowcast flash flood hotspots with lead times of 4 to 6 hours.27 Google's internal Flood Hub utilizes a similar but distinct dual-model AI approach. Google deploys a Hydrologic Model to forecast the sheer volume of water flowing in a river based on API data, which subsequently feeds an Inundation Model. The Inundation Model simulates how that specific volume of water will move across the floodplain, predicting localized water depth and spread up to 7 days in advance.28 By adopting these machine learning paradigms, the backend can generate probabilistic flood boundary polygons in real-time, strictly driven by data rather than physical sensors.

## **Backend Architecture: FastAPI and Elasticsearch Geospatial Processing**

The backend infrastructure must serve a highly demanding dual purpose: executing massive streams of geospatial predictive models and simultaneously managing thousands of concurrent mobile client WebSocket connections. This necessitates a highly decoupled, asynchronous, and scalable microservices architecture.

### **FastAPI and Asynchronous Task Queue Management**

FastAPI has emerged as the optimal Python framework for the API layer due to its native, built-in support for ASGI (Asynchronous Server Gateway Interface), allowing for rapid, non-blocking I/O operations.29 In a real-time mobile notification system relying on geographic coordinates, keeping the main event loop unblocked is paramount to maintaining low latency.30

However, a critical architectural decision involves the handling of heavy computational tasks. When a weather API detects an intense precipitation anomaly, it triggers the execution of the XGBoost predictive model, the generation of Shapely polygons, and the spatial simplification of those geometries. Executing these CPU-bound operations directly within a FastAPI route or utilizing FastAPI's built-in BackgroundTasks class is structurally flawed for this scale. BackgroundTasks execute within the exact same event loop that serves the application's incoming web requests.31 If the server crashes or restarts, any tasks queued in BackgroundTasks are permanently lost, leading to catastrophic failures in delivering emergency alerts.32

Therefore, the architecture delegates all heavy geospatial processing and machine learning inference to a Celery distributed task queue, typically backed by a Redis or RabbitMQ message broker.31 Celery provides robust, independent worker management, automatic retry mechanisms for failed API calls, and horizontal scaling capabilities across multiple server nodes.34 FastAPI is strictly relegated to managing the lightweight ingestion of mobile client GPS locations, user authentication, dispatching tasks to the Celery broker, and subsequently streaming the processed alert notifications back to the mobile clients via WebSockets or Server-Sent Events (SSE).35 This separation of concerns ensures that the API remains highly responsive even when the system is mathematically modeling a massive regional flood event.

### **Elasticsearch Geospatial Indexing and ES|QL**

Elasticsearch serves as the primary geospatial database engine, inherently designed for massive horizontal scale and rapid document retrieval. Within the Elasticsearch index mapping, the historical blue zones (derived from satellite data) and the live, dynamically predicted flood extents are stored as geo\_shape fields.38 The geo\_shape data type is engineered to accommodate highly complex geometries, including Point, LineString, Polygon, and MultiPolygon.39 Conversely, the real-time locations of the mobile application users are stored as simple geo\_point fields, representing single latitude and longitude coordinate pairs.40

To determine if users are located within a hazardous zone, the backend must execute complex spatial intersection queries. The recent introduction of the Elasticsearch Query Language (ES|QL) drastically simplifies this implementation, allowing data engineers to write SQL-like spatial joins.38 For example, locating all user documents within a newly generated flood polygon utilizes the ST\_INTERSECTS function directly within the ES|QL pipeline, providing significant performance optimizations over the legacy Query DSL API, particularly when combined with spatial aggregations.41

### **The Percolator Paradigm for Real-Time Geofencing**

While standard spatial queries—searching an index of static polygons using a dynamically updating user point—are functionally sound, they are computationally ruinous for continuous, real-time tracking of tens of thousands of moving mobile users. Constantly updating millions of user location documents and repeatedly querying them against flood zones will inevitably bottleneck the database cluster. To achieve true real-time alerting without crushing the database architecture, the system reverses the traditional search paradigm utilizing the Elasticsearch **Percolator** feature.42

Instead of indexing user coordinates as static documents and querying them against flood zones, the system registers each user's location (or their immediate localized geofence) as a *persistent query* stored within a specialized .percolator index.42 As the user moves, their application updates their stored query.

When the Celery worker generates a new active flood polygon based on the weather APIs, this single GeoJSON polygon document is "percolated" against the index containing thousands of user queries. Elasticsearch evaluates the newly injected document and instantly returns the unique IDs of every user whose pre-registered query mathematically intersects with the flood polygon.42 Because the Percolator requires the entire query index to be loaded into active system memory (RAM), it is highly optimized for extreme execution speed.42

To further enhance the linear execution speed of the percolation phase and prevent evaluating the polygon against users on the other side of the planet, standard Elasticsearch metadata filters (e.g., routing based on geographic region IDs or country codes) can be applied. This isolates the spatial evaluation strictly to users residing in the affected geographical quadrant, reducing the computational load by orders of magnitude.45

## **Dynamic Routing and Evacuation Logistics**

When a mobile user is caught near an actively expanding flood zone, static GPS navigation systems (which rely on historical speed limits and standard road networks) are rendered instantly obsolete. Directing a user through a flooded intersection is a life-threatening failure. The application must dynamically calculate evacuation routes that actively and algorithmically avoid the hazardous blue zones and the live inundation polygons.

This capability is achieved by integrating dynamic routing engines such as OpenRouteService (ORS), Mapbox, or GraphHopper directly into the backend architecture.46 OpenRouteService's Directions API offers a specific, highly valuable avoid\_polygons parameter that accepts standard GeoJSON multipolygon formatting.48 When an evacuation route request is generated by the user's device, the FastAPI backend intercepts the request and injects the currently active flood polygons into this parameter.49

The underlying routing algorithm represents the road network as a mathematical graph. Upon receiving the avoid\_polygons payload, the engine intersects the polygons with the graph edges (street segments). It temporarily deletes or applies an infinite cost weight to any edge that overlaps with the flooded polygons. The engine then computes Dijkstra's or the A\* shortest path algorithm using only the remaining, safe topological network.49 Mapbox and GraphHopper achieve similar results by dynamically adjusting custom edge weights, completely circumventing hazardous intersections.50

However, system architects must carefully manage the mathematical complexity of the GeoJSON passed to the routing engine. Standard HTTP GET requests have strict character length limits. Passing a highly detailed, unoptimized flood polygon containing tens of thousands of vertices will cause the request to exceed header limits and fail entirely.49 Therefore, the Celery backend must aggressively utilize the Douglas-Peucker algorithm via the Shapely library to simplify the polygon geometries (e.g., using a high spatial tolerance value) before submitting the routing request. This ensures the GeoJSON payload remains lightweight while preserving the overarching boundary of the hazard, ensuring robust, rapid, and successful pathfinding for the end user.

## **Emergency Response Flow: Technical Feasibility and OS Constraints**

The most legally and technically critical feature proposed in the user query is the emergency response flow: initiating an automatic, zero-touch action if a user opens the application within an active flood zone. The query proposes two distinct actions to protect a trapped user: automatically dialing the local emergency dispatch number (e.g., 911 in North America, 112 in the European Union), or dispatching peer-to-peer push notifications to nearby app users.

### **The Legal and Technical Impossibility of Automated Emergency Dialing**

The concept of a third-party mobile application automatically initiating a cellular call to emergency services without explicit user intervention is technically prohibited by deep mobile operating system architectures and heavily restricted by international telecommunications laws.

**Android Operating System Constraints:**

The Android OS strictly partitions and monitors call intents to prevent malicious behavior. While standard third-party applications can utilize the ACTION\_DIAL intent to open the system phone dialer pre-populated with a phone number, the user is strictly required to manually tap the physical "Call" button on the screen to connect the call. To bypass the system dialer UI and initiate a call directly in the background, an application must use the ACTION\_CALL intent.

However, the Android framework explicitly and universally prohibits ACTION\_CALL from dialing known emergency numbers (such as 911, 112, 999\) for any third-party application downloaded from the Google Play Store.53 Dialing emergency services directly requires the CALL\_PRIVILEGED permission flag. According to core Android documentation, this permission is "Not for use by third-party applications".5 It is restricted exclusively to system-level apps signed with the device manufacturer's cryptographic key and installed directly on the protected system partition.53

Attempts by developers to circumvent this restriction via Android's AccessibilityService—an API powerful enough to programmatically mimic user screen taps and navigate the UI on behalf of the user—are strictly monitored and blocked by Google Play. While the AccessibilityService can technically perform deterministic UI automation 54, Google's developer policies strictly forbid its use for autonomous execution by non-accessibility tools. Only verified applications explicitly designed to assist users with physical disabilities (and flagged internally with the isAccessibilityTool="true" attribute) are permitted to use these APIs for automation.55 Utilizing this API for an emergency flood app violates the core policy, resulting in immediate app suspension and developer account termination.

Furthermore, automated emergency calls risk violating telecommunications regulations, such as Kari's Law and E9-1-1 technical architectures.57 E9-1-1 systems route calls based on physical cell tower signaling and triangulation, not the GPS data held within a third-party app.57 Automated dialing risks creating accidental denial-of-service (DoS) attacks against local Public Safety Answering Points (PSAPs), prompting regulators to demand OS-level blocks.57 While Android's Radio HAL 1.4 includes specific handling for Automatically Initiated eCalls (AIEC), this is reserved for embedded hardware systems (like automotive crash sensors), not downloadable mobile apps.59

**iOS Operating System Constraints:** Apple's iOS environment is equally, if not more, restrictive. To initiate a phone call from an iOS application, developers utilize the UIApplication.shared.open(\_:options:completionHandler:) method combined with a formatted tel: scheme URL.60

Prior to older iOS versions, this specific method could initiate calls immediately without a user prompt. However, to permanently prevent abuse, malicious routing, and mistaken emergency calls, Apple modified the iOS core to intercept all tel: URLs. The operating system now universally forces a non-bypassable system-level alert dialogue requiring the user to tap "Call" or "Cancel" before the cellular radio engages.61 Developers cannot bypass, hide, or programmatically click this confirmation dialogue, especially for emergency digit strings. In highly controlled enterprise scenarios, developers have attempted to build temporary 10-second grace period UIs, but the iOS system ultimately and inescapably enforces the final manual confirmation tap.61

**Conclusion on Auto-Dialing:** Because of the stringent E9-1-1 technical architectures and Kari's Law 57, the mobile operating systems entirely lock down programmatic access to emergency dispatch. The proposed automated dialing flow is technically impossible for public distribution. The application must instead utilize a highly visible, one-tap "Emergency SOS" button that formats the number and opens the native system dialer, transferring the final execution to the human user.

### **Viable Alternative: Peer-to-Peer Push Notification Warnings**

Because automatic 911 dialing is unachievable, the application must rely on the alternative flow: a localized, high-speed push notification architecture to alert nearby users that a person is physically trapped and requires immediate civilian or coordinated assistance.

When the user opens the application within a designated active flood polygon, the app transmits an SOS payload containing their exact geo\_point to the FastAPI backend. The backend immediately triggers a high-priority Celery task. This task queries the Elasticsearch database utilizing an ST\_DISTANCE function 38 to find all currently active or recently active application users within a specific, survivable radius (e.g., 2 kilometers).

To ensure the guaranteed delivery of these critical SOS messages, the system cannot rely on standard push notifications, which are frequently delayed by battery optimization algorithms or silenced by the user. The architecture must utilize the highest priority messaging classes provided by Apple and Google:

1. **Apple Push Notification service (APNs) \- Critical Alerts:** Standard iOS notifications are suppressed when the device is muted, locked, or placed in a "Do Not Disturb" or "Sleep" Focus mode. To bypass this entirely, the application must apply for and be granted a specific, highly restricted Apple entitlement (com.apple.developer.usernotifications.critical-alerts).62 Critical Alerts are designed explicitly for security systems, water leak sensors, and extreme weather.4 They play a loud, distinct audio sound and appear permanently on the lock screen regardless of the user's volume or mute settings.4 Securing this entitlement requires a rigorous manual review process by Apple engineers, proving the application is a legitimate health, safety, or public security tool.  
2. **Firebase Cloud Messaging (FCM) \- High Priority:** On the Android ecosystem, developers must construct backend notification payloads with the priority explicitly set to high and categorize them appropriately. While Android gives users granular control over notification channels, properly configured emergency alert channels can bypass Do Not Disturb rules if the user is prompted to grant the application the appropriate override permissions during onboarding.3

By leveraging persistent WebSockets for real-time delivery when the app is active in the foreground, and APNs Critical Alerts/FCM High-Priority push notifications when the app is backgrounded or the device is locked, the system guarantees that nearby citizens are rapidly and aggressively mobilized to assist the trapped user.

## **Legal Framework and Liability Mitigation**

The deployment of a crisis management and predictive disaster application carries immense legal exposure for the developers and the maintaining organization. Because the system utilizes statistical machine learning predictions and third-party meteorological APIs rather than absolute physical ground truths, false positives and—much more dangerously—false negatives are inevitable.64

If the XGBoost model fails to predict a flash flood due to anomalous radar scatter, or if the Elasticsearch percolator experiences a Redis queue delay during a mass-casualty event, users relying exclusively on the application for safety may be severely harmed. To mitigate this extreme liability, robust, legally binding disclaimers must be embedded deeply within the application's User Experience (UX) and the End-User License Agreement (EULA).65

1. **Strict Limitation of Liability Clauses:** The EULA must explicitly state that the application is provided "as is," without any warranties of absolute accuracy. A definitive legal clause must be established capping or entirely waving the developer's liability for death, bodily injury, or property damage resulting from reliance on the application's dynamic routing, flood mapping, or peer-to-peer alerting.67  
2. **Informational Purpose Disclaimer:** Users must acknowledge via a mandatory click-through agreement that the tool is strictly supplemental to official government and meteorological agency warnings. It does not replace national E9-1-1 dispatch guidance or federally mandated public broadcast systems, such as the Wireless Emergency Alerts (WEA) broadcast from cell towers by FEMA and the FCC.68  
3. **No Guarantee of Uninterrupted Service:** The EULA should prominently specify that the developers retain the right to suspend, rate-limit, or terminate services without notice. This protects the organization legally in the event of unforeseen server expenditures, catastrophic API outages, or cloud infrastructure failures during a major disaster.67

## **Strategic Synthesis and Architectural Recommendations**

Engineering a real-time, street-level flood warning system that operates entirely without physical hardware sensors is computationally demanding but technically viable utilizing modern geospatial and machine learning stacks.

By analyzing Copernicus Sentinel SAR data using bi-temporal U-Net architectures to map historical blue zones, and relying on robust providers like Meteomatics and OpenWeatherMap for live, high-resolution meteorological telemetry, the system can utilize ensemble models like XGBoost to forecast flash flood boundaries with exceptional accuracy. The backend must strictly isolate the intensive processing of these boundaries. While FastAPI excels at managing concurrent API connections and WebSocket streams, it must delegate the heavy geospatial computations to a robust Celery and Redis task queue to prevent event-loop blocking.

Elasticsearch acts as the central engine of the spatial architecture. Utilizing the Percolator index inverts standard database queries, allowing the system to instantly map rapidly expanding flood polygons against tens of thousands of stationary and moving user geofences in memory, enabling near-instantaneous alerting pipelines.

However, the ambition to fully automate emergency dialing must be permanently abandoned. Android and iOS strictly guard the CALL\_PRIVILEGED intent and standard tel: protocols to prevent automated abuse, DoS attacks on PSAPs, and to comply with E9-1-1 telecom regulations. Attempting to force automation through accessibility services explicitly violates developer policies and guarantees the application's removal from global app stores.

Instead, the emergency response flow must pivot entirely toward a localized, peer-to-peer notification system. By securing Apple's Critical Alerts entitlement and Android's high-priority push channels, the application can bypass OS-level "Do Not Disturb" silences, alerting nearby community members of trapped users effectively and safely. Supported by dynamic routing engines like OpenRouteService to guide both victims and rescuers around active hazards using simplified GeoJSON polygons, this architectural blueprint establishes a highly resilient, globally scalable, and legally sound crisis management platform.
